# Coffee: Origins and Global Cultivation

Coffee is one of the world's most widely consumed beverages, with origins rooted in the highlands of Ethiopia. According to a legend that has circulated since at least the fifteenth century, a goat herder named Kaldi observed his animals becoming unusually energetic after eating red berries from a particular shrub. Whether or not the story is historically accurate, Ethiopia is firmly established as the native home of Coffea arabica, the species that accounts for the majority of commercially grown coffee today.

From Ethiopia, knowledge of the coffee plant spread across the Red Sea to the Arabian Peninsula. Yemen became the first country to cultivate coffee as an agricultural crop and to trade it commercially, beginning in the fifteenth century. For roughly two hundred years, Yemeni merchants maintained near-total control of the global coffee trade through the port of Mocha — a city whose name was borrowed by later coffee culture to describe a style of preparation blending coffee and chocolate. The Arabians also developed the practice of roasting and brewing the beans in a form closely resembling modern preparation.

Coffee reached Europe during the seventeenth century, where it spread with remarkable speed. London alone had over two thousand coffeehouses by 1700. These establishments, sometimes called "penny universities" because a penny paid for entry and a cup, became centers of political debate, financial dealing, and intellectual exchange. Lloyd's of London, the insurance market, began as a coffeehouse frequented by sailors and merchants. Several newspapers and financial institutions trace their origins to coffeehouse culture.

The Dutch were among the first Europeans to obtain live coffee plants, establishing plantations on the island of Java in present-day Indonesia around 1696. From the Dutch East Indies the plant traveled to the Caribbean and then to Latin America, where conditions proved exceptionally favorable. Brazil became the world's largest coffee producer by the nineteenth century, a position it has never relinquished; it currently accounts for roughly one-third of global output.

Cultivation today is concentrated in the "Coffee Belt," the equatorial band between the Tropics of Cancer and Capricorn. Leading producers include Brazil, Vietnam, Colombia, Indonesia, Ethiopia, and Honduras. Two species dominate commercial production: Coffea arabica, valued for its complex flavor and moderate caffeine content, and Coffea canephora (robusta), preferred for its higher caffeine levels, disease resistance, and lower growing cost.

Approximately 2.25 billion cups of coffee are consumed worldwide every day, supporting an industry worth over $100 billion annually. Specialty coffee — emphasizing single-origin beans, transparent supply chains, and precise brewing — has grown into a significant segment alongside the mass-market trade.
